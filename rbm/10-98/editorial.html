Editor's Note

  Recently, I began working on a new program. The program's goal was to do a list of operations over the internet. These included downloading, PPP Up, PPP Down, Wait Until <time>, and the likes.

  In most other programming languages, this would have been a huge pain to write. First of all, I'd have to write the batch program and interface itself, which would be fairly hard. Then, I'd have to write in HTTP and FTP transfer capabilities. In itself, each of these would have taken hours, if not days, and would have involved reading long, boring documents in search of one piece of information.

  Thank god for Realbasic! Thanks to RB's great class system, and the many available socket classes (in this case, httpSocket and RB FTP), I would have been able to write the entire program without knowing what a socket was! And even though I do know, I was still saved from countless hours of coding and debugging thanks to classes.

  This left me rather excited, and I began to realize that this whole scenario sounded familiar. Recognize it? Sure you do. It's OpenDoc!

  Yes, Real Software has succeeded in places that Apple did not. And they didn't even try to!

  Think about it. With OpenDoc, you took components, and put different ones into documents based on what you needed to get done. In RB, you take classes and plugins, and put them into different projects based on what you need.

  In fact, classes in RB have an added benefit that OpenDoc components (sorry, Live Objects) didn't: they're usually open source. This allows people to integrate them into their programs more tightly, and even take out parts they don't need.

  If you take this whole concept to the extreme, when there are classes for everything, making an application could become as simple as throwing a few classes into a project, and putting together an interface. MUCH easier than writing the whole thing from scratch!

  As RB becomes more powerful, and more classes for more and more purposes become available, RB will become an even easier and faster language. By using other people's classes in your program, you're basically getting a whole slew of developers for whatever your project is. Heck, you could even release updates to your program without even writing a single new line of code.

  Odd as this sounds, it's certainly true now, and will only become more so in the future.

- Dan Vanderkam
